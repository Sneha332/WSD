About Us

The Milky Way galaxy, home to our solar system, is a majestic spiral galaxy with an estimated diameter of 100,000 light-years. It contains 100 to 400 billion stars and features a complex structure of spiral arms, a central bulge, and a halo of dark matter. Its diverse stellar population includes everything from ancient stars to young, massive ones.

The Andromeda Galaxy, also known as M31, is the closest spiral galaxy to the Milky Way and is expected to collide with our galaxy in about 4.5 billion years. This collision will likely result in the formation of a new, larger galaxy. The Andromeda Galaxy is approximately 2.5 million light-years away and contains around one trillion stars.

Spiral galaxies, such as the Milky Way and Andromeda, are distinguished by their rotating disks of stars and nebulae, with spiral arms extending from a central bulge. These galaxies often have a spherical halo of dark matter and contain vast interstellar gas and dust clouds. The spiral arms are regions of intense star formation and cosmic activity.
